# Study 02 — Dimensions of Play-Hunger

*Organon: dimensional analysis. Study 01 listed motives as points; this study finds the
axes the points lie along. A good dimension sorts many motives at once and predicts
something. Each axis gets: poles, a gloss, and a prediction it licenses.*

---

## D1. Toward ⟷ Away

**Seeking a positive (curiosity, mastery, beauty) vs. fleeing a negative (boredom, pain, dread).**
The single most diagnostic axis. Toward-play energizes; away-play anesthetizes. The same
game serves both — the same farm in Stardew is an adventure at 19 and a painkiller at 38.
*Prediction:* away-players are loyal but joyless; they churn suddenly and completely when
the pain source resolves — or when the game demands effort, since effort breaks anesthesia.

## D2. Arousal-Up ⟷ Arousal-Down

**Play as stimulant vs. play as sedative.** Horror and ranked ladder on one pole; match-3
and farming sims on the other. Most players hold a portfolio spanning both and self-medicate
by time of day.
*Prediction:* the "evening slot" of a player's life is usually arousal-down; games that
demand peak arousal compete for a much smaller daypart than their designers assume.

## D3. Self-Facing ⟷ Other-Facing

**Playing for a private experience vs. playing for/with/against an audience.** Solitaire vs.
stream sniping. Note the hybrid: the solo player narrating an imagined audience ("chat would
love this") — other-facing play with no others present.
*Prediction:* other-facing motives survive bad games (people endure mediocre games for good
company); self-facing motives don't.

## D4. In-Game ⟷ Through-Game

**Wanting what's inside the game vs. what the game produces outside itself** — status,
friendship, streaming income, a topic for lunch, a merit badge of identity. Through-game
players look like devoted fans and quit the instant the through-line breaks (the friend
group dissolves; the game stops being culturally current).
*Prediction:* retention built on through-game motives is rented, not owned.

## D5. Process ⟷ Outcome

**The playing is the point vs. the having-played is the point.** Journey-lovers replay;
outcome-lovers finish, shelve, and cite. Completionism is outcome-orientation metastasized;
sandbox dwelling is process-orientation with the outcome surgically removed.
*Prediction:* outcome players tolerate grind (it's a price); process players experience
grind as betrayal (it's the product, and the product is bad).

## D6. Certainty ⟷ Uncertainty (the wager axis)

**How much unknowing the player wants to purchase.** Too certain = boredom; too uncertain
= noise. Every genre is a certainty-dosage: puzzle games sell *eventual* certainty, roguelikes
sell *renewable* uncertainty, gacha sells uncertainty about *whether you'll be rewarded at all*.
*Prediction:* a player's tolerance-band narrows when tired; the same person needs blackjack
at noon and a walking sim at midnight.

## D7. Effort ⟷ Grace

**Wanting to earn it vs. wanting to be given it.** The Souls player and the god-mode player.
Grace isn't laziness: being handed unearned power (a broken combo, an overpowered drop) is
its own distinct delight — the windfall thrill.
*Prediction:* games that never grant grace feel puritan; games that never require effort
feel patronizing. The craving oscillates *within* one session.

## D8. Immersion ⟷ Observation

**Being inside the world vs. studying the system from above.** The role-player names their
horse; the optimizer sees the horse as a +12% movement modifier. Same game, orthogonal joys.
*Prediction:* UI is the battleground of this axis — every overlay serves observers and
wounds immersers.

## D9. Order-Making ⟷ Chaos-Making

**Tidying the world vs. perturbing it.** Inventory sorters, base planners, fog clearers vs.
physics griefers, sequence breakers, "what if I put the bucket on his head" players.
Both are experiments in agency; one composes, one decomposes.
*Prediction:* the chaos-maker is the order-maker's canary — when your most destructive
players get bored, the possibility space is exhausted for everyone; the tidiers just
haven't noticed yet.

## D10. Fidelity ⟷ Abstraction

**Wanting the world thick (simulation, texture, weather) vs. thin (chess, tetrominoes,
pure rule).** Thick worlds are inhabited; thin worlds are *thought*. Nobody roleplays a rook.
*Prediction:* thin games age like mathematics; thick games age like special effects.

## D11. Volition ⟷ Compulsion

**Choosing to play vs. being unable not to.** The axis every other axis can slide down.
Any motive — even beauty, even friendship — can calcify into compulsion; the phenomenology
flips from "I get to" to "I have to" while the behavior looks identical from outside.
*Prediction:* players self-report position on this axis accurately and *retroactively* —
they know a game had them only after it lets go. Churn surveys measure autopsies.

## D12. Sanctioned ⟷ Transgressive

**Playing as approved leisure vs. playing as minor rebellion.** For the child up past
bedtime and the employee with the boss-key, transgression is not a side effect but a
flavoring — the game tastes better *because* it's stolen.
*Prediction:* fully legitimizing play (gamified work, mandated fun) strips this seasoning
and can kill an appetite it meant to feed.

## D13. Repetition ⟷ Novelty

**The comfort of the known loop vs. the spark of the new thing.** Not a preference but a
metabolism: every player converts novelty into familiarity at some rate, and needs
resupply on some schedule. Live-service games are novelty logistics operations.
*Prediction:* the players who consume content fastest are the loudest and least
representative; balancing for them starves the repetition-metabolizers who quietly
constitute the base.

## D14. Alone-Together ⟷ Together-Alone

**A subtler social axis than D3:** playing solo *in company* (same couch, parallel
Stardew farms, silent guildmates as ambient presence) vs. playing together *in isolation*
(matchmade teammates you'll never meet twice). Much modern play is alone-together:
presence without demand.
*Prediction:* ambient co-presence (see: fishing quietly near a friend) is massively
underserved relative to demand; most multiplayer design assumes people want *interaction*
when many want *company*.

## D15. Rehearsal ⟷ Vacation

**Play as preparation for life vs. play as leave from it.** The practice-theory pole
(learning, training, simulating) against the sabbath pole (uselessness as the point).
This axis runs *underneath* the whole inquiry: it is the ancient disagreement between
Groos ("play is for something") and the child ("play is play").
*Prediction:* players resent being told their vacation is secretly rehearsal
("this game teaches problem-solving!") almost as much as being told their rehearsal
is merely vacation ("it's just a game").

---

## Observations

- **D1 (Toward/Away) and D11 (Volition/Compulsion) form a 2×2 that may be the master
  diagram of the whole project:**

  |  | Volitional | Compulsive |
  |---|---|---|
  | **Toward** | *Passion* — the healthy ideal | *Obsession* — the speedrunner at 4am |
  | **Away** | *Respite* — legitimate medicine | *Refuge-trap* — the state dark patterns farm |

  A player's journey with one game often walks this square clockwise from Passion.
  Flag for Study 06 (transformations).

- Several axes are really about *dosage*, not type (D2, D6, D13): the player is a
  homeostat, and the game is an instrument for holding an inner variable in a band.
  This suggests a thesis: **people don't play to feel good; they play to feel
  *regulated*.** Flag for synthesis.

- D15 dissolves rather than sorts: most play is *both* rehearsal and vacation, and the
  question "which really?" may be malformed — like asking whether sleep is for dreaming
  or resting.
