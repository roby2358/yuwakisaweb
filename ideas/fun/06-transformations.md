# Study 06 — Transformations: The "Why" Is a Moving Target

*Organon: temporal morphology. "Why do people play?" assumes one answer per person per
game. False: the motive migrates. This study tracks the mutation of "why" at three
timescales — the session (minutes), the career (a player's life with one game), and
the lifespan (a life with play itself).*

---

## I. The Session Scale (minutes to hours)

A single session's motive typically walks a chain:

1. **Ignition** (minute 0): almost never intrinsic. The trigger is situational — a
   free half-hour (#106), the 9pm groove (#84), a friend's ping, an itch from the
   quest log (#83). *People rarely start playing because play is wonderful; they start
   because starting is easy.*
2. **Re-entry** (minutes 1–10): finding the thread again. Rituals here (#73): check the
   base, sort the inventory, warm-up match. Motive: continuity of self (#66).
3. **Absorption** (variable): flow if lucky (#72), engagement if not. The motive during
   absorption is *nothing* — this is the phenomenological heart of it: at play's best
   there is no "why" present in the mind at all. Motive is what you see on the way in
   and the way out, never during.
4. **The pivot** (mid-session): the original aim completes or stalls, and a session
   continues under new management: "I'll just also..." The pivot is where sessions
   overextend — motive A's session running on motive H's fumes (one more box, #119).
5. **Exit** (or failure to): good exits ride a completed arc (quest done, rank up —
   leave on an up-beat); bad exits are timeouts, guilt (#34), or the mirror moment
   (#35). **The feeling at exit — not during play — is what's remembered and what
   decides the next ignition.** (Peak-end rule; design accordingly: the last 90
   seconds of a session buy the next one.)

## II. The Career Scale (a player's life with one game)

The archetypal motive-migration, with the Study 02 master-square (Passion / Obsession /
Respite / Refuge-trap) as the map:

- **Stage 1 — Courtship.** Motives: curiosity (#11), beauty (#89), a friend's testimony.
  The game is all questions. Fragile: one bounced hour ends it (Study 03 §II).
- **Stage 2 — Honeymoon.** The learning gradient at maximum steepness (#12); epiphanies
  daily (#20). *Toward + Volitional: Passion corner.* This stage is what people mean
  by "fun," and it cannot last by definition — its fuel is ignorance, which burns.
- **Stage 3 — The Settling.** Novelty metabolized (D13); loops familiar; motive quietly
  hands off from discovery to **competence** (#16, #28) and **ritual** (#73). Most
  honest games end careers here as graduation (#18). Games that continue past this
  point continue on *new* motives, or on hooks.
- **Stage 4 — The Fork.** Three roads out of the Settling:
  - **Deepening** → mastery culture: optimization (#16), style (#94), the community
    of the standard (P13). Motive is now self-measurement (P2). *Passion, renewed
    lease — but drifting toward the Obsession corner at 4am.*
  - **Socializing** → the game becomes the *place* (P9, P12); motive is now the people
    (D4 through-game). The game can decline in quality for years without losing
    these players — until the guild dies (#20).
  - **Sedation** → the game becomes wallpaper: podcast-and-grind, hands busy (#5, #44).
    *Away + Volitional: Respite corner* — legitimate medicine, but one step from...
- **Stage 5 — The Grip** (not everyone; the dark stage). Volition flips to compulsion
  (D11) while behavior looks unchanged. Streaks (#81), sunk cost (#80), social
  hostage-taking (#86). *Refuge-trap corner.* The tell: the player stops telling
  stories about the game. Nothing is happening in there; it merely continues.
- **Stage 6 — The Ending.** Every career gets one (Study 03 §III): graduation,
  completion, betrayal, the seen hook, the job-flip, the body's veto, or the quiet
  backlog coup. **The ending's emotional signature is permanent** — it decides the
  game's place in memory, the review score in the heart, and whether returns (below)
  are possible.
- **Stage 7 — Afterlife.** Careers don't always stay ended: the *return* is its own
  phenomenon. Returns are triggered by expansion news, a friend restarting, nostalgia
  (P9), or life echoing the original wound the game once treated (D1). Returning
  players re-enter at Stage 3, not Stage 2 — the honeymoon is unrepeatable, and games
  that greet returners with tutorials insult them (A7's gym-return problem).

**Career-scale law:** *each stage's motive is invisible to the previous stage.* The
courtship player cannot imagine grinding; the settled player cannot remember what was
confusing; the gripped player cannot see the grip. Design and criticism constantly err
by assuming one stage's psychology for all stages. (Player surveys sample whoever's
still there — mostly Stages 3–5 — then puzzle over bounced Stage-1 players they never met.)

## III. The Lifespan Scale (a life with play itself)

- **Early childhood:** play *is* cognition — no frame, no license needed, no
  play/not-play boundary yet. The question "why does the child play?" is malformed,
  like "why does the child think?"
- **Middle childhood:** rules discovered as *toys themselves* — the age of made-up
  games with seventeen amendments ("you can't use lava twice!"). Legislation as play;
  the birth of the magic circle as a *negotiated* thing (Bateson's signal learned).
- **Adolescence:** play goes social-vertical — status ladders (#48), testimony (P13),
  identity brickwork (#64). The game is an arena for the project of mattering.
- **Early adulthood:** the license crisis (Study 03, #3): time collapses, and play must
  now *justify itself* against career and love. Play either goes underground (stolen
  sessions, D12), goes social-horizontal (the standing game night as friendship's
  load-bearing wall), or goes dormant.
- **Parenthood:** play's great inversion — from doing it to *hosting* it: the parent
  as designer of another's magic circle, rediscovering play from the far side of the
  desk (A9). Own play compresses into interstitial forms (#106) — the age of the
  phone game, judged as decline, actually adaptation.
- **Middle age:** the returns begin (Stage 7 en masse): remasters, retro consoles,
  the guild reunion. Play as time machine — not to the game, to the *self* (P9).
  Simultaneously, mastery-hunger often mellows into stewardship (#75): modding,
  curating, teaching (#53).
- **Elderhood:** play as cognitive gardening (the daily crossword as A11), as
  structure for emptied schedules (#110), as bridge to grandchildren (the inversion
  inverts again — hosted *by* the child now). And at the end, play's oldest function
  returns: *the domestication of finitude* (#71) — practicing endings in a medium
  where endings are survivable.

**Lifespan-scale law:** play never disappears; it *re-costumes*. The person who
"stopped gaming" now plays the stock market, fantasy football, Wordle, office
politics, or their garden. Play-hunger is conserved; only its socially licensed
container changes. (Confirms Study 03 #10: non-players mostly don't exist.)

## Harvest

- **Motive is stage-dependent, and stages are mutually invisible.** The question
  "why do people play?" has a different true answer at every stage — most disputes
  about play (in criticism, in design, at family dinners) are stage confusions.
- **Absorption has no motive.** At its center, play is motive-free; "why" exists only
  at the boundaries. Any theory built solely on in-the-moment experience will find
  nothing there and conclude play is empty — the emptiness *is* the product (P10).
- **Exits write memory; memory writes ignitions.** The engine of retention is not the
  session but the *seam between sessions*. The last 90 seconds and the ending stage
  are the highest-leverage design surfaces in games — and the least designed.
- **Returns re-enter at the Settling, never the Honeymoon.** An entire design genre
  ("welcome back" flows) is missing from most games.
